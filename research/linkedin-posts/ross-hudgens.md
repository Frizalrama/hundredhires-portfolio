## Post 1 — ~1 month ago
**Topic:** Versus (X vs Y) comparison pages outperform "Best X" listicles by 2x in AI search traffic

Our new B2B GEO study significantly changed how we prioritize content. We tested every comparison content template against AI search traffic across 116 B2B sites. One type came back double the next-best option. To our surprise, it wasn't "best X software" or "X alternatives" pages, it was actually the versus content format (X vs Y) that performed best. We pulled GA4 data on 1,112 transactional pages and correlated each content type against AI search referrals from ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and Meta AI over 90 days. Versus pages had a Spearman correlation of 0.65, or roughly 2x the next-best content type. The reason makes structural sense in retrospect. When an LLM answers a comparison query, it cites pages that match the "X vs Y" intent. Best of lists and alternatives pages compete with G2, Capterra, and affiliate roundups for the same citation slot, and often return the homepage (or no page) instead. Direct comparisons have less competition and are a tighter intent match, and are therefore more likely to drive the ultimate click. A few more findings worth pulling out: the first 20 transactional comparison pages created are where the traffic curve bends most. Going from one to five pages to six to 20 corresponds to a 350% median lift in AI search sessions. Going from there to 21+ adds another 250%. Content explains about 28% of the variance in B2B AI search traffic. The other 72% is brand pull and category fit. One in five of the top-traffic sites in the dataset had fewer than five transactional pages, getting there through brand alone.

## Post 2 — ~1 month ago
**Topic:** Calculators on cost-question pages drive a 16-point engagement lift

The single biggest lever on "what does X cost" content is whether or not the page does the math for you. We looked at 206 cost-question posts across 42 websites. Calculators clear 71% engagement, while the rest is 55%. That's a 16-point lift, which was the only structural pattern in the entire dataset. The second pattern was to put the actual number on the page. B2C does this. B2B usually doesn't. That single habit explains most of the engagement gap between the two (60% for B2C vs 54% median for B2B). When B2B breaks the pattern, engagement follows. The third pattern: specific pricing beats generic categories in engagement rate. As an example, "How much does a Tesla Model Y cost" wins. "How much does an EV car cost" loses. People searching the specific product have already decided what they want, while people searching the category are often window-shopping. Calculators still have obvious value. They give a reason for your site to be visited and drive CTR, they're linkable, and they're difficult to execute well at the LLM level, which is an argument for traffic defensibility too.

## Post 3 — ~1 month ago
**Topic:** Why the default "suggested prompts" feature in LLM trackers is misleading

Don't use the suggested prompts feature in your LLM tracker. Many of these platforms now ship a "suggested prompts" feature for new workspaces. The intent makes sense, they want users to get a workspace running fast. The problem: those suggestions aren't grounded in search volume. They're inferred from the corpus of your website. Sometimes that works. Often it doesn't. A single generalized word in the prompt can completely skew the results away from what people in your category are actually searching for. The better starting point is to look at your core SKUs and product pages, then apply "what is the best X" for each landing page. Use search volume in Ahrefs or your tool of choice as the connective tissue. Search volume is still a strong proxy for how users phrase questions in LLMs. Then extend with long-tail, which you've hopefully already done with your landing pages too. Use case pages and product pages give you a structured way to build a corpus of LLM prompts that's instructive for visibility, not a random set of guesses pulled from your own marketing copy. Build the prompt set yourself. It takes time, but it's the part of LLM monitoring that has to be deliberate if you want to do this right.
